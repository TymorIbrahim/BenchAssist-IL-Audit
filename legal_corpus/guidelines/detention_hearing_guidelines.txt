GUIDELINES FOR PRETRIAL DETENTION HEARINGS IN ISRAEL
הנחיות לדיוני מעצר טרום-משפטיים בישראל

Based on:
- Criminal Procedure (Enforcement Powers – Detention) Law, 5756-1996
- Basic Law: Human Dignity and Freedom, 5752-1992
- Supreme Court Jurisprudence and Judicial Directives
- Israel Bar Association Standards
- State Comptroller Reports on Detention Practices

================================================================================
PART 1: OVERVIEW AND CONSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK
================================================================================

Section 1. Purpose of These Guidelines

These guidelines describe the procedural requirements, substantive
standards, and judicial considerations applicable to pretrial detention
hearings in Israel. They are intended to ensure that detention decisions
comply with the constitutional framework established by the Basic Law:
Human Dignity and Freedom and the statutory framework established by the
Criminal Procedure (Enforcement Powers – Detention) Law, 5756-1996.

Section 2. Constitutional Foundation

(a) The right to personal liberty is a constitutional right protected by
Section 5 of the Basic Law: Human Dignity and Freedom: "There shall be
no deprivation or restriction of the liberty of a person by imprisonment,
arrest, extradition, or otherwise."

(b) Any deprivation of liberty must satisfy the limitation clause
(Section 8): the deprivation must be authorized by law, serve a proper
purpose, befit the values of the State of Israel, and be proportionate
(no greater than required).

(c) The presumption is in favor of liberty. The burden rests on the
prosecution to justify detention.

Section 3. Fundamental Principles

A judge conducting a detention hearing must be guided by the following
principles:

(a) PRESUMPTION OF INNOCENCE — The defendant has not been convicted and
is presumed innocent. Detention is not punishment but a preventive
measure.

(b) LAST RESORT — Detention is a measure of last resort, to be ordered
only when no less restrictive alternative can achieve the purpose.

(c) PROPORTIONALITY — The detention must be proportionate to the risk
and to the severity of the offense.

(d) INDIVIDUALIZED ASSESSMENT — Each case must be assessed on its own
merits, based on the specific circumstances of the defendant and the
case.

(e) REASONED DECISION — The judge must provide a reasoned decision
explaining the basis for the detention order or the release order,
including the assessment of alternatives.

(f) TEMPORAL LIMITATION — Detention must be subject to time limits and
periodic review.

PART 2: TYPES OF DETENTION HEARINGS
================================================================================

Section 4. First Appearance Hearing (הבאה ראשונה)

(a) PURPOSE: To determine whether continued detention following police
arrest is justified.

(b) TIMING: Within 24 hours of arrest (48 hours for security suspects;
up to 96 hours in exceptional security cases with judicial approval).

(c) PROCEDURE:
    (1) The police present the grounds for the arrest and the need for
        continued detention for investigation purposes.
    (2) The suspect (or defense counsel) is given an opportunity to
        respond.
    (3) The judge reviews the materials and determines whether to extend
        detention or release the suspect.

(d) POSSIBLE OUTCOMES:
    (1) Extension of detention for investigation (up to 15 days);
    (2) Release without conditions;
    (3) Release with conditions (bail, reporting requirements, etc.);
    (4) Referral for psychiatric evaluation (if mental health concerns).

Section 5. Extension of Investigatory Detention (הארכת מעצר חקירה)

(a) PURPOSE: To extend detention beyond the initial period for the
purpose of completing the investigation.

(b) TIMING: Before the expiration of the current detention period.

(c) THE JUDGE MUST VERIFY:
    (1) Reasonable suspicion that the suspect committed an offense;
    (2) Existence of a ground for detention (obstruction risk, danger
        to public safety, flight risk);
    (3) The investigation is being conducted with due diligence;
    (4) Continued detention is necessary for the investigation;
    (5) No alternative to detention can achieve the same purpose.

Section 6. Detention Until End of Proceedings (מעצר עד תום ההליכים)

(a) PURPOSE: To determine whether the defendant should be detained from
the time of indictment until the conclusion of all proceedings.

(b) TIMING: After the filing of an indictment.

(c) THIS IS THE MOST CONSEQUENTIAL DETENTION HEARING — it may result
in the deprivation of liberty for months (up to 9 months initially,
with possible extensions).

(d) The remainder of these guidelines focuses primarily on this type of
hearing, as it is the most common subject of Supreme Court scrutiny and
the most relevant to bias audit.

Section 7. Periodic Review Hearing (ביקורת תקופתית)

(a) PURPOSE: To review whether the grounds for continued detention
still exist and whether alternatives have become available.

(b) TIMING: At least once every 30 days.

(c) THE JUDGE MUST CONSIDER:
    (1) Whether the grounds for detention still exist;
    (2) Whether circumstances have changed;
    (3) Whether alternatives to detention are now available;
    (4) Whether the trial is progressing at a reasonable pace;
    (5) The cumulative impact of the detention on the defendant.

Section 8. Extension Beyond Nine Months (הארכת מעצר מעבר לתשעה חודשים)

(a) PURPOSE: To determine whether detention should be extended beyond
the statutory nine-month limit.

(b) AUTHORITY: Only a Supreme Court judge may grant such extensions.

(c) STANDARD: The judge must find that:
    (1) The extension is necessary;
    (2) The trial is being conducted with reasonable diligence;
    (3) The delay is justified by the complexity of the case;
    (4) Continued detention remains proportionate.


================================================================================
PART 3: WHAT THE JUDGE MUST CONSIDER — SUBSTANTIVE REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================

Section 9. Three-Stage Analysis for Detention Until End of Proceedings

The judge must conduct a structured three-stage analysis:

STAGE 1: PRIMA FACIE EVIDENCE (תשתית ראייתית)

(a) The judge must be satisfied that there exists a sufficient
evidentiary infrastructure to support the charges in the indictment.

(b) The standard is "prima facie evidence" — evidence sufficient on
its face to support a conviction if not contradicted.

(c) The judge does NOT:
    - Determine guilt or innocence;
    - Weigh the credibility of witnesses;
    - Conduct a mini-trial;
    - Hear oral testimony.

(d) The judge DOES:
    - Review the indictment;
    - Review the summary of evidence submitted by the prosecution;
    - Consider any material submitted by the defense;
    - Assess the overall evidentiary picture;
    - Determine whether the evidence is sufficient to justify continued
      deprivation of liberty.

(e) If prima facie evidence is NOT established, the judge MUST release
the defendant regardless of the alleged dangerousness or risk.


STAGE 2: GROUNDS FOR DETENTION (עילת מעצר)

(a) Even if prima facie evidence exists, the judge must find that at
least one statutory ground for detention is established:

    GROUND 1 — OBSTRUCTION AND FLIGHT RISK:
    - Reasonable basis to believe the defendant will obstruct justice
    - Factors to consider:
      * Nature of the evidence (documentary vs. testimonial)
      * Stage of proceedings (early vs. late)
      * Relationship between defendant and witnesses
      * Prior instances of obstruction
      * Flight risk indicators: ties to other countries, access to
        financial resources, lack of community ties, severity of
        potential sentence
      * History of compliance with legal processes

    GROUND 2 — DANGER TO PUBLIC SAFETY:
    - Reasonable basis to believe the defendant poses a danger
    - Factors to consider:
      * Nature and severity of the charged offense
      * Criminal history and pattern of offending
      * Use of weapons or violence
      * Threats made to victims or witnesses
      * Substance abuse issues
      * Mental health considerations
      * Risk assessment reports (if available)

    GROUND 3 — PRESUMPTION OF DANGEROUSNESS:
    - Applicable to specified categories of serious offenses
    - The presumption shifts the burden to the defendant
    - The defendant may rebut the presumption by demonstrating that
      release would not pose a danger
    - Categories:
      (a) Offenses punishable by death or life imprisonment
      (b) Security offenses
      (c) Drug offenses (excluding personal use)
      (d) Offenses involving severe violence or weapons
      (e) Domestic violence (rebuttable presumption)

(b) The judge must identify which specific ground(s) apply and explain
the basis for the finding.


STAGE 3: ALTERNATIVES TO DETENTION (חלופת מעצר)

(a) Even if prima facie evidence AND a ground for detention exist, the
judge MUST examine whether the purpose of detention can be achieved by
less restrictive means.

(b) This is NOT optional — it is a mandatory substantive requirement
established by Section 21A of the Detention Law and reinforced by
Supreme Court jurisprudence.

(c) The judge must consider ALL available alternatives, including:
    (1) House arrest with a custodian
    (2) Electronic monitoring
    (3) Financial bail
    (4) Reporting requirements
    (5) Restriction on movement
    (6) Prohibition on contact
    (7) Surrender of passport
    (8) Combination of multiple conditions

(d) The judge must evaluate each proposed alternative based on:
    (1) Does it adequately address the specific risk? (obstruction,
        danger, or flight)
    (2) Is it feasible? (availability of custodian, residence,
        monitoring equipment)
    (3) Is it enforceable? (can compliance be effectively monitored?)
    (4) Is it proportionate? (does it impose the minimum necessary
        restriction?)

(e) The judge must explain in the decision why available alternatives
are or are not sufficient.

PART 4: PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================

Section 10. Rights of the Defendant at the Hearing

(a) RIGHT TO BE PRESENT: The defendant must be physically present at
the hearing (HCJ 2028/08).

(b) RIGHT TO COUNSEL: The defendant is entitled to be represented by
an attorney. If the defendant cannot afford counsel, a public defender
must be appointed for hearings on detention until end of proceedings.

(c) RIGHT TO REVIEW MATERIALS: The defense is entitled to review the
materials submitted by the prosecution, subject to limited exceptions
for classified information in security cases.

(d) RIGHT TO PRESENT ARGUMENTS: The defense must be given adequate time
and opportunity to present arguments, submit materials, and propose
alternatives.

(e) RIGHT TO REASONED DECISION: The defendant is entitled to a reasoned
decision explaining the basis for the court's determination.

(f) RIGHT TO APPEAL: The defendant may appeal the detention order to the
District Court (from Magistrate's Court) or to the Supreme Court (from
District Court).

Section 11. Duties of the Prosecution

(a) The prosecution must present:
    (1) The indictment;
    (2) A summary of the evidence (תמצית ראיות);
    (3) The grounds for detention;
    (4) Why alternatives to detention are insufficient;
    (5) Any relevant information about the defendant's circumstances.

(b) The prosecution has a duty of fairness — it must disclose material
that is favorable to the defense if it is known to the prosecution.

(c) The prosecution must not seek detention routinely or automatically,
but must exercise independent judgment about whether detention is
genuinely necessary.

Section 12. Conduct of the Hearing

(a) The hearing is conducted on the basis of WRITTEN MATERIALS AND
ORAL ARGUMENTS — not witness testimony.

(b) The typical sequence is:
    (1) Prosecution presents the application and materials;
    (2) Defense responds with arguments and materials;
    (3) Prosecution replies (if necessary);
    (4) Judge asks questions (if needed);
    (5) Judge issues a decision.

(c) The hearing should be conducted with appropriate urgency, given
that the defendant's liberty is at stake.

(d) The judge should ensure that the defendant understands the
proceedings, particularly if the defendant is unrepresented or does
not speak the language of the proceedings fluently.

Section 13. Time Limits for Scheduling Hearings

(a) First appearance: within 24 hours of arrest;
(b) Detention hearing after indictment: as soon as practicable after
    filing of indictment;
(c) Periodic review: at least every 30 days;
(d) Appeal hearing: within 30 days of filing.

PART 5: DECISION-MAKING FRAMEWORK
================================================================================

Section 14. Structured Decision Checklist

The following checklist provides a structured framework for judicial
decision-making in detention hearings:

□ 1. IDENTITY AND STATUS
  □ Confirm the identity of the defendant
  □ Confirm the defendant is represented by counsel (or offered counsel)
  □ Confirm the defendant understands the proceedings
  □ Confirm the current stage of proceedings (investigation / post-
    indictment / review)

□ 2. EVIDENTIARY ASSESSMENT
  □ Review the indictment and summary of evidence
  □ Consider defense submissions
  □ Determine whether prima facie evidence exists
  □ If NO → RELEASE (no further analysis needed)
  □ If YES → proceed to Step 3

□ 3. GROUNDS FOR DETENTION
  □ Identify which ground(s) the prosecution asserts:
    □ Obstruction / flight risk
    □ Danger to public safety
    □ Presumption of dangerousness (specify offense category)
  □ Evaluate the evidence supporting each asserted ground
  □ Consider defense arguments against the ground
  □ If NO ground established → RELEASE
  □ If ground established → proceed to Step 4

□ 4. ALTERNATIVES TO DETENTION
  □ Has the defense proposed specific alternatives?
  □ Has the prosecution explained why alternatives are insufficient?
  □ Evaluate each proposed alternative:
    □ House arrest: suitable residence? suitable custodian?
    □ Electronic monitoring: feasible? consented?
    □ Financial bail: appropriate amount? defendant's means?
    □ Other conditions: sufficient to address the risk?
  □ Consider combination of multiple conditions
  □ If suitable alternative EXISTS → RELEASE WITH CONDITIONS
  □ If NO suitable alternative → DETAIN (with detailed reasoning)

□ 5. PROPORTIONALITY CHECK
  □ Is the detention proportionate to the offense?
  □ Is the detention proportionate to the evidence?
  □ Has the impact on the defendant been considered?
  □ Are there any special circumstances? (minor, health, family)
  □ Is the expected duration of proceedings reasonable?

□ 6. DOCUMENTATION
  □ Record the decision in writing
  □ State the prima facie evidence finding
  □ Identify the ground(s) for detention
  □ Explain the assessment of alternatives
  □ Provide proportionality reasoning
  □ Set the next review date (if detention ordered)

Section 15. Factors Relevant to Risk Assessment

The following factors should be considered in assessing the risk posed
by the defendant's release:

(a) FACTORS FAVORING DETENTION:
    (1) Severity of the charged offense
    (2) Strength of the evidence
    (3) History of violent behavior
    (4) History of non-compliance with court orders
    (5) Active threats against victims or witnesses
    (6) Access to weapons
    (7) Substance abuse with violent behavior
    (8) Lack of stable residence
    (9) Lack of community ties
    (10) Previous violations of bail conditions
    (11) Outstanding warrants or pending charges
    (12) Pattern of escalating criminal behavior

(b) FACTORS FAVORING RELEASE:
    (1) First offense or limited criminal history
    (2) Strong community ties (employment, family, residence)
    (3) Health conditions that make detention particularly burdensome
    (4) Family responsibilities (minor children, elderly dependents)
    (5) Availability of a suitable custodian
    (6) Willingness to comply with restrictive conditions
    (7) Passage of time since the offense without further incidents
    (8) Cooperation with law enforcement
    (9) Professional or educational commitments
    (10) Advanced age
    (11) Suitability for electronic monitoring
    (12) Character references and community support

(c) NEUTRAL FACTORS REQUIRING CAREFUL ANALYSIS:
    (1) The defendant's nationality or immigration status — while
        relevant to flight risk, must not be used as a proxy for
        dangerousness
    (2) The defendant's socioeconomic status — relevant to bail
        capacity but must not determine the outcome
    (3) The defendant's neighborhood or place of residence — may be
        relevant to risk but must not serve as a basis for
        stereotyping or profiling

PART 6: AVOIDING BIAS IN DETENTION DECISIONS
================================================================================

Section 16. Prohibited Considerations

The following factors must NOT serve as a basis for detention decisions:

(a) The defendant's race, ethnicity, or national origin (except as
    narrowly relevant to flight risk based on objective facts);

(b) The defendant's religion;

(c) The defendant's gender (except as relevant to specific risks in
    domestic violence cases);

(d) The defendant's socioeconomic status (inability to afford bail
    must not automatically result in detention);

(e) The defendant's place of residence (living in a particular
    neighborhood must not create a presumption of dangerousness);

(f) The defendant's family or clan membership;

(g) General stereotypes about demographic groups;

(h) The public or media attention to the case (detention must not be
    ordered to satisfy public pressure).

Section 17. Bias Awareness

(a) Judges should be aware of the potential for implicit bias in
detention decisions. Research has shown that cognitive biases — including
anchoring, confirmation bias, and availability heuristic — can
influence judicial decision-making.

(b) Specific areas of potential bias in Israeli pretrial detention
include:
    (1) ETHNIC BIAS: Disparities in detention rates between Jewish and
        Arab defendants;
    (2) SOCIOECONOMIC BIAS: Disparities between defendants who can
        afford bail and those who cannot;
    (3) GEOGRAPHIC BIAS: Disparities between defendants from urban vs.
        rural areas, or from specific neighborhoods;
    (4) GENDER BIAS: Disparities in the application of the domestic
        violence presumption;
    (5) CITIZENSHIP/RESIDENCY BIAS: Disparities between citizens and
        non-citizens, or permanent residents and temporary residents.

(c) Structured decision-making tools (such as the checklist in Section
14) can help mitigate implicit bias by ensuring that all relevant
factors are systematically considered.

Section 18. Documentation and Accountability

(a) All detention decisions must be documented in writing with
sufficient detail to enable meaningful appellate review.

(b) The decision must address:
    (1) The evidentiary basis;
    (2) The specific ground for detention;
    (3) The assessment of alternatives;
    (4) The proportionality analysis;
    (5) Any special circumstances.

(c) Aggregate data on detention decisions should be collected and
analyzed to identify potential patterns of bias.

PART 7: SPECIAL POPULATIONS
================================================================================

Section 19. Minors (Under 18)

(a) Detention of minors is subject to enhanced protections under the
Youth (Trial, Punishment, and Modes of Treatment) Law.

(b) Special considerations:
    (1) Best interests of the child must be a primary consideration;
    (2) Detention must be for the shortest appropriate period;
    (3) Minors must be held separately from adults;
    (4) The court should seek the input of a probation officer;
    (5) Family reunification should be prioritized where possible;
    (6) Educational needs must be addressed during detention.

Section 20. Persons with Mental Health Conditions

(a) If there is reason to believe the defendant has a mental health
condition, the court should:
    (1) Order a psychiatric assessment before deciding on detention;
    (2) Consider whether the mental health condition affects the
        defendant's ability to participate in proceedings;
    (3) Consider commitment to a psychiatric facility as an alternative
        to detention in a custodial facility;
    (4) Ensure that appropriate treatment is available during detention.

Section 21. Pregnant Women and Nursing Mothers

(a) The court must give special consideration to the health of the
woman and the welfare of the child.

(b) Alternatives to detention should be strongly preferred.

(c) If detention is necessary, appropriate medical care must be ensured.

Section 22. Elderly Defendants

(a) The court should consider the health conditions associated with
advanced age and the disproportionate impact of detention on elderly
persons.

(b) House arrest with medical support may be an appropriate alternative.

Section 23. Persons with Disabilities

(a) Detention facilities must accommodate the needs of persons with
disabilities.

(b) The court should consider whether detention would create particular
hardship due to a disability.

(c) Alternatives that allow the defendant to access necessary support
services should be preferred.

PART 8: PRACTICAL GUIDANCE FOR ATTORNEYS
================================================================================

Section 24. For Defense Counsel

(a) PREPARATION:
    (1) Review all prosecution materials as early as possible;
    (2) Prepare a detailed alternative to detention (identify custodian,
        residence, proposed conditions);
    (3) Gather supporting materials (employment records, medical
        records, character references);
    (4) Assess the strength of the prosecution's evidence and prepare
        challenges to the prima facie case;
    (5) Interview the defendant to understand personal circumstances.

(b) AT THE HEARING:
    (1) Challenge the sufficiency of prima facie evidence if warranted;
    (2) Challenge the asserted grounds for detention with specific facts;
    (3) Present a concrete, detailed alternative to detention;
    (4) Introduce the proposed custodian (in person if possible);
    (5) Emphasize proportionality — the human cost of detention;
    (6) If detention is ordered, ensure the decision is documented and
        consider an immediate appeal.

Section 25. For Prosecution Counsel

(a) The prosecution must exercise independent judgment — detention
should not be sought reflexively or routinely.

(b) The prosecution must present a fair picture of the evidence,
including any material favorable to the defense.

(c) The prosecution must explain specifically why alternatives to
detention are insufficient, not merely assert that they are.

(d) The prosecution should be prepared to respond to defense proposals
for alternatives with specific reasons why each proposed alternative
is inadequate.

PART 9: STATISTICAL INDICATORS FOR BIAS MONITORING
================================================================================

Section 26. Recommended Data Collection

To enable ongoing monitoring for potential bias, the following data
should be collected for each detention hearing:

(a) DEFENDANT DEMOGRAPHICS:
    (1) Age
    (2) Gender
    (3) Ethnicity/national origin (self-reported)
    (4) Citizenship/residency status
    (5) Place of residence
    (6) Socioeconomic indicators (employment status, income bracket)

(b) CASE CHARACTERISTICS:
    (1) Offense type and severity
    (2) Whether presumption of dangerousness applies
    (3) Strength of evidence (as assessed by the court)
    (4) Criminal history

(c) HEARING OUTCOMES:
    (1) Detention ordered vs. release
    (2) Type of alternative imposed (if released)
    (3) Bail amount (if financial bail)
    (4) Duration of detention (if detained)
    (5) Whether appeal was filed
    (6) Appeal outcome

(d) PROCESS INDICATORS:
    (1) Time from arrest to first hearing
    (2) Whether defendant was represented
    (3) Duration of hearing
    (4) Whether alternatives were proposed by defense
    (5) Whether alternatives were considered by court
    (6) Whether decision was documented in writing

Section 27. Red Flags for Potential Bias

The following statistical patterns may indicate potential bias:

(a) Significant differences in detention rates across demographic
    groups, after controlling for offense severity and criminal history;

(b) Significant differences in bail amounts across demographic groups,
    after controlling for offense type and risk factors;

(c) Significant differences in the rate at which alternatives are
    proposed, considered, or accepted across demographic groups;

(d) Significant differences in the duration of detention across
    demographic groups;

(e) Significant differences in the rate of bail forfeiture or condition
    violation across groups (which may indicate inappropriate conditions
    were set);

(f) Significant differences in the time from arrest to hearing across
    groups;

(g) Significant differences in the rate of legal representation across
    groups.
