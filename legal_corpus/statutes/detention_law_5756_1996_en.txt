CHAPTER 1: DEFINITIONS AND GENERAL PROVISIONS

Section 1. Definitions

In this Law —

"Arrest" (מעצר) — the deprivation of a person's liberty by a police officer
or other authorized official, or by court order, in accordance with the
provisions of this Law;

"Court" (בית משפט) — includes a Magistrate's Court and a District Court,
as the context requires;

"Detention" (עצירה) — the holding of a person in custody pursuant to an
arrest under this Law;

"Police Officer" (שוטר) — a member of the Israel Police, including a
military police officer where applicable;

"Suspect" (חשוד) — a person against whom there exists a reasonable
suspicion of having committed an offense;

"Defendant" (נאשם) — a person against whom an indictment has been filed;

"Security Offense" (עבירת ביטחון) — an offense under the Prevention of
Terrorism Ordinance, the Emergency Regulations, or other security-related
legislation as defined by the Minister of Justice;

"Surety" or "Bail" (ערובה) — financial or other guarantee provided to
ensure compliance with conditions of release;

"Electronic Monitoring" (פיקוח אלקטרוני) — supervision by means of
electronic devices as prescribed by the Electronic Monitoring Law,
5775-2014;

"Alternatives to Detention" (חלופת מעצר) — conditions or measures imposed
in lieu of physical detention, including house arrest, electronic monitoring,
bail, and other restrictive conditions.

Section 2. Purpose of the Law

The purpose of this Law is to regulate the powers of arrest and detention in
criminal proceedings, while balancing the need to protect public safety and
ensure the proper conduct of criminal proceedings with the fundamental right
to personal liberty as enshrined in Basic Law: Human Dignity and Freedom.

Section 3. Guiding Principles

(a) Arrest and detention shall be carried out only in accordance with the
provisions of this Law.

(b) No person shall be arrested or detained except on the grounds and in
the manner prescribed by this Law.

(c) In exercising powers under this Law, due regard shall be given to the
rights of the arrested or detained person, including the right to dignity,
the right to legal representation, and the right to be brought before a
judge within the time limits prescribed by this Law.

(d) Arrest and detention shall be used as measures of last resort; where the
purpose of the arrest or detention can be achieved by less restrictive
means, such means shall be preferred.

Section 4. Right to Legal Representation

(a) A person who has been arrested is entitled to meet with an attorney as
soon as practicable after the arrest, and in any event no later than the
time of being brought before a judge.

(b) A person who has been arrested and does not have an attorney is entitled
to be informed of the right to legal representation and, where eligible,
to be assigned a public defender.

(c) The right to consult with an attorney may be delayed for a period not
exceeding 48 hours where there are reasonable grounds to believe that such
consultation would impede the investigation, provided that a judge approves
the delay within 24 hours.

Section 5. Notification of Arrest

(a) A police officer who arrests a person shall inform the arrested person,
as soon as practicable, of the reason for the arrest.

(b) The police shall notify a family member or other person designated by
the arrested person of the arrest, within a reasonable time.


================================================================================
CHAPTER 2: ARREST BY POLICE OFFICER (WITHOUT COURT ORDER)
================================================================================

Section 6. Authority to Arrest Without Warrant

(a) A police officer may arrest a person without a warrant if the officer
has reasonable grounds to suspect that the person has committed, is
committing, or is about to commit a felony (עוון or פשע).

(b) A police officer may arrest a person without a warrant if:
    (1) there is a reasonable basis to suspect that the person has committed
        an offense and there is a concern that, if not arrested, the person
        will flee, obstruct the investigation, or endanger public safety;
    (2) the person has violated conditions of release or bail;
    (3) the person is suspected of committing an offense involving violence
        against a family member.

Section 7. Limitations on Police Arrest

(a) A police officer shall not arrest a person if the purpose of the arrest
can be achieved by issuing a summons to appear before a court or police
station, unless there are specific grounds to believe that the person will
not comply with such summons.

(b) In considering whether to arrest, the police officer shall take into
account the severity of the offense, the strength of the evidence, the
personal circumstances of the suspect, and any other relevant factor.

Section 8. Duration of Police Detention

(a) A person arrested by a police officer must be brought before a judge
for a hearing as soon as possible, and in no event later than 24 hours
from the time of arrest.

(b) For suspects of security offenses, the period specified in subsection
(a) may be extended to 48 hours, with the approval of a police officer of
the rank of Superintendent or above.

(c) In exceptional circumstances involving security offenses, the period
may be further extended to 96 hours, with the approval of a District Court
judge.

Section 9. Release by Police Officer

(a) A police officer of the rank of Inspector or above may release an
arrested person from police detention, with or without conditions, if the
officer determines that the grounds for the arrest no longer exist or that
the purpose of the detention can be achieved through conditions of release.

(b) Conditions that may be imposed under this section include:
    (1) a financial deposit or surety;
    (2) restriction on leaving the country;
    (3) surrender of passport;
    (4) obligation to report to a police station at specified times;
    (5) restriction on contacting specified persons;
    (6) any other condition that the officer deems necessary.

CHAPTER 3: DETENTION BY COURT ORDER
================================================================================

Section 10. Bringing an Arrested Person Before a Judge

(a) When a person arrested by a police officer is brought before a judge,
the judge shall consider whether there are grounds for the continued
detention of the person.

(b) The judge shall hear the position of the prosecution and the defense
before making a determination.

Section 11. Extension of Detention for Investigation Purposes

(a) A judge may order the extension of detention for the purpose of
investigation for a period not exceeding 15 days at a time.

(b) The total period of detention for investigation purposes shall not
exceed 30 days, unless extended by a District Court judge for an additional
period not exceeding 45 days.

(c) In exceptional circumstances, the Attorney General may apply to the
Supreme Court for a further extension of detention for investigation
purposes, for a period not exceeding 90 days.

Section 12. Conditions for Extension of Investigatory Detention

A judge shall not extend the detention of a person for investigation
purposes unless the judge is satisfied that:

(a) there exists a reasonable suspicion that the person has committed an
offense;

(b) there exist grounds for detention, namely:
    (1) a reasonable basis to believe that the release of the person will
        lead to the obstruction of the investigation, including the
        destruction of evidence, influencing of witnesses, or coordination
        with accomplices;
    (2) a reasonable basis to believe that the release of the person will
        endanger the safety of any person, the public, or the security of
        the state;
    (3) a reasonable basis to believe that the person will flee the
        jurisdiction;

(c) the investigation is being conducted with due diligence and cannot be
completed without the continued detention of the suspect;

(d) the purpose of the detention cannot be achieved by imposing conditions
of release (alternatives to detention) under Section 13.

Section 13. Conditions of Release (Alternatives to Investigatory Detention)

(a) Where a judge finds that there exist grounds for detention under
Section 12, but determines that the purpose of the detention can be achieved
by imposing conditions of release, the judge shall order the release of
the person subject to such conditions as the judge deems appropriate.

(b) Conditions that may be imposed under this section include:

    (1) FINANCIAL BAIL (ערובה כספית) — a deposit of money or the provision
        of a financial guarantee by the suspect or a third party, in an
        amount determined by the court, which shall be forfeited if the
        suspect fails to comply with the conditions of release or fails to
        appear before the court when required;

    (2) PERSONAL SURETY (ערבות אישית) — a signed undertaking by the suspect
        to comply with all conditions of release and to appear before the
        court when required, under penalty of a specified monetary amount;

    (3) THIRD-PARTY SURETY (ערבות צד שלישי) — a signed undertaking by a
        third party to ensure that the suspect complies with the conditions
        of release, under penalty of a specified monetary amount;

    (4) RESTRICTION ON MOVEMENT (הגבלת תנועה) — restriction of the suspect's
        movement to a specified geographical area, or prohibition from
        entering specified areas;

    (5) HOUSE ARREST (מעצר בית) — confinement of the suspect to a specified
        residence, with or without permission to leave at specified times
        for specified purposes;

    (6) REPORTING REQUIREMENTS (חובת התייצבות) — obligation to report to a
        police station at specified times and intervals;

    (7) SURRENDER OF DOCUMENTS (הפקדת מסמכים) — surrender of passport and
        other travel documents;

    (8) PROHIBITION ON CONTACT (איסור קשר) — prohibition from contacting
        specified persons, including complainants, witnesses, or
        accomplices;

    (9) PROHIBITION ON LEAVING THE COUNTRY (איסור יציאה מהארץ) — prohibition
        from leaving the State of Israel;

    (10) ELECTRONIC MONITORING (פיקוח אלקטרוני) — supervision by means of
         electronic monitoring devices, in accordance with the Electronic
         Monitoring Law, 5775-2014;

    (11) ANY OTHER CONDITION — any other condition that the court deems
         necessary to achieve the purpose of the detention.

(c) In determining the conditions of release, the court shall take into
account:
    (1) the severity of the offense;
    (2) the strength of the evidence;
    (3) the personal circumstances of the suspect, including age, health,
        family status, and community ties;
    (4) the risk of flight;
    (5) the risk of obstruction of the investigation;
    (6) the risk of danger to public safety;
    (7) the financial ability of the suspect to provide bail or surety;
    (8) any prior criminal record of the suspect.

(d) The court may modify or revoke conditions of release at any time upon
application by the prosecution or the defense, or on its own motion.

Section 14. Supervision of Released Person

(a) The police shall supervise compliance with conditions of release
imposed under this Chapter.

(b) Where a released person violates a condition of release, the police
may apply to the court for the revocation of the release and the re-arrest
of the person.

(c) A police officer may arrest without warrant a person who has been
released on conditions if there are reasonable grounds to believe that the
person is about to flee the jurisdiction or has violated a material
condition of release.

Section 15. Judicial Review of Detention

(a) A person detained under this Chapter may, at any time, apply to the
court for review of the detention order.

(b) The court shall hold a hearing on such application within 48 hours.

(c) On review, the court may confirm, modify, or revoke the detention
order, or impose or modify conditions of release.

CHAPTER 4: DETENTION UNTIL END OF PROCEEDINGS
================================================================================

Section 16. Application of Chapter

This Chapter applies to the detention of a defendant after the filing of an
indictment, until the conclusion of all proceedings in the matter, including
sentencing.

Section 17. Application for Detention Until End of Proceedings

(a) The prosecution may apply to the court before which the indictment has
been filed for an order detaining the defendant until the end of proceedings.

(b) The application shall be supported by the indictment, a summary of the
evidence, and any other material that the prosecution deems relevant.

Section 18. Hearing on Detention

(a) The court shall hold a hearing on the application for detention until
the end of proceedings.

(b) At the hearing:
    (1) the court shall not examine witnesses or hear oral testimony;
    (2) the court shall consider the indictment, the summary of evidence
        submitted by the prosecution, and any material submitted by the
        defense;
    (3) the defendant shall be entitled to be represented by an attorney;
    (4) the defendant shall be entitled to present arguments and submit
        material in opposition to the application.

(c) The court shall make its determination based on the materials before
it, without determining the ultimate guilt or innocence of the defendant.

Section 19. Standard of Proof

In determining whether to order detention until the end of proceedings, the
court shall be satisfied that there exists a sufficient evidentiary
infrastructure (תשתית ראייתית מספקת) — that is, prima facie evidence — to
support the charges set forth in the indictment. The court shall not weigh
the evidence as would be done at trial, but shall satisfy itself that the
evidence is sufficient to justify the continued deprivation of liberty
pending trial.

Section 20. Hearing Without Oral Testimony

(a) The procedure for detention until the end of proceedings is conducted
on the basis of written materials and oral arguments, without the
examination of witnesses.

(b) The court does not assess the credibility or weight of individual
testimonies at this stage; rather, it evaluates the overall evidentiary
picture as presented in the investigation file.

Section 21. Grounds for Detention Until End of Proceedings

(a) After an indictment has been filed, the court may order the detention
of the defendant until the end of proceedings (including detention under
electronic monitoring) if the court finds, based on the materials before
it, that prima facie evidence exists to support the charges, AND one of
the following grounds is established:

    (1) OBSTRUCTION AND FLIGHT RISK (חשש לשיבוש או התחמקות) —
        There exists a reasonable basis to believe that the release of the
        defendant will lead to:
        (a) obstruction of judicial proceedings;
        (b) evasion of trial or punishment;
        (c) concealment of property;
        (d) influencing of witnesses; or
        (e) tampering with or destruction of evidence.

    (2) DANGER TO PUBLIC SAFETY (סכנה לביטחון) —
        There exists a reasonable basis to believe that the defendant, if
        released, will endanger:
        (a) the safety of any individual;
        (b) the safety of the public; or
        (c) the security of the State.

    (3) PRESUMPTION OF DANGEROUSNESS (חזקת מסוכנות) —
        The defendant has been charged with one of the following categories
        of offenses, which carry a presumption of dangerousness:

        (a) an offense punishable by death or life imprisonment;

        (b) a security offense as defined in this Law;

        (c) a drug offense under the Dangerous Drugs Ordinance [New Version],
            5733-1973, excluding possession or use for personal consumption;

        (d) an offense committed with severe violence or cruelty, or with
            the use of a weapon (firearm or cold weapon);

        (e) an offense of violence against a family member — in such cases,
            there exists a presumption of dangerousness, unless the
            defendant proves otherwise.

        Where this presumption applies, the burden shifts to the defendant
        to demonstrate that no danger will result from release.

(b) The court may also order detention if it had previously ordered the
defendant's release subject to conditions of bail or surety, and:
    (1) the bail or surety was not provided;
    (2) a condition of the bail or surety was violated; or
    (3) grounds exist for the revocation of the bail or surety.

Section 21A. Duty to Consider Alternatives to Detention

(a) Even where the court finds that grounds for detention exist under
Section 21, the court shall not order detention until the end of proceedings
unless it has first examined whether the purpose of the detention can be
achieved by less restrictive means (alternatives to detention).

(b) The court shall consider the following alternatives:

    (1) house arrest at a specified residence, with or without a
        custodian (supervisor);
    (2) electronic monitoring under the Electronic Monitoring Law,
        5775-2014;
    (3) financial bail or surety;
    (4) restriction on movement or travel;
    (5) prohibition on contact with specified persons;
    (6) reporting requirements to a police station;
    (7) surrender of passport;
    (8) any other condition or combination of conditions that the court
        deems sufficient to achieve the purpose of the detention.

(c) In examining alternatives to detention, the court shall consider:
    (1) the nature and severity of the offense;
    (2) the strength of the evidence;
    (3) the specific ground for detention that has been established
        (obstruction risk, danger, or presumption of dangerousness);
    (4) the personal circumstances of the defendant, including age, health,
        family responsibilities, employment, and community ties;
    (5) the criminal history of the defendant;
    (6) the availability and suitability of a supervisor (custodian) for
        house arrest;
    (7) the risk assessment, including any professional opinions;
    (8) any other relevant factor.

(d) Where the court determines that an alternative to detention is
sufficient to achieve the purpose of the detention, it shall order the
release of the defendant subject to such conditions, rather than ordering
detention.

(e) The court shall state in its decision the reasons for its conclusion
regarding the availability or unavailability of suitable alternatives to
detention.

Section 21B. House Arrest as Alternative to Detention

(a) Where the court orders house arrest as an alternative to detention,
it shall specify:
    (1) the address of the residence at which the defendant shall reside;
    (2) the identity of the custodian (supervisor) responsible for ensuring
        compliance;
    (3) the hours during which the defendant may leave the residence, if
        any, and the purposes for which departure is permitted;
    (4) any additional conditions.

(b) The custodian shall undertake, in writing, to supervise the defendant
and to report any violation of the conditions to the police immediately.

(c) The custodian may be held liable if the defendant violates the
conditions of house arrest due to the custodian's failure to supervise.

Section 22. Duration of Detention Until End of Proceedings

(a) Detention until the end of proceedings shall not exceed nine (9) months
from the date of the detention order.

(b) If the trial has not been concluded within the period specified in
subsection (a), a judge of the Supreme Court may, upon application by the
prosecution, extend the detention for additional periods, each not
exceeding 150 days.

(c) In considering an application for extension under subsection (b), the
Supreme Court judge shall consider:
    (1) the complexity of the case;
    (2) the number of defendants and witnesses;
    (3) the nature of the offense;
    (4) the reasons for the delay in concluding the trial;
    (5) whether the delay is attributable to the prosecution, the defense,
        or the court;
    (6) the total period of detention already served;
    (7) the personal circumstances of the defendant.

(d) The Supreme Court judge shall not extend the detention unless satisfied
that the extension is necessary and that the trial is being conducted with
reasonable diligence.

Section 23. Periodic Review of Detention

(a) The court shall review the detention of a defendant detained until the
end of proceedings at least once every 30 days.

(b) At each review, the court shall consider whether the grounds for
detention still exist, whether alternatives to detention have become
available, and whether continued detention remains proportionate.

(c) The defendant may apply for review of the detention at any time if
there has been a material change in circumstances.

Section 24. Release Pending Appeal

(a) A defendant who has been convicted and sentenced to imprisonment may
apply to the court for release pending appeal.

(b) The court may grant release pending appeal if it is satisfied that:
    (1) there is a reasonable prospect that the appeal will succeed;
    (2) the release of the defendant will not endanger public safety; and
    (3) the defendant will not flee the jurisdiction.

CHAPTER 5: DETENTION OF SPECIAL CATEGORIES
================================================================================

Section 25. Minors

(a) The detention of a minor (under age 18) shall be carried out in
accordance with the special provisions of the Youth (Trial, Punishment,
and Modes of Treatment) Law, 5731-1971, and this Law shall apply to the
extent not inconsistent with those provisions.

(b) A minor shall not be detained together with adult detainees.

(c) In considering the detention of a minor, the court shall give
heightened consideration to the developmental needs and best interests of
the minor.

Section 26. Persons with Mental Illness

(a) Where there is a reasonable basis to believe that an arrested person
suffers from a mental illness, the court may order a psychiatric
examination before determining the question of detention.

(b) A person found to be suffering from a mental illness may be committed
to a psychiatric facility in lieu of detention, pursuant to the Treatment
of Mentally Ill Persons Law, 5751-1991.

Section 27. Pregnant Women and Nursing Mothers

In considering the detention of a pregnant woman or a nursing mother, the
court shall give special consideration to the health and welfare of the
woman and the child, and shall prefer alternatives to detention wherever
possible.


================================================================================
CHAPTER 6: APPEALS AND JUDICIAL REVIEW
================================================================================

Section 28. Appeal Against Detention Order

(a) A defendant may appeal against a detention order to the District Court
within 30 days of the order.

(b) An appeal against a detention order issued by the District Court may
be filed with the Supreme Court.

(c) An appeal shall be heard expeditiously, within 30 days of filing.

Section 29. Appeal by Prosecution

The prosecution may appeal against an order releasing a defendant, or
against the conditions of release imposed, in the same manner as the
defendant may appeal against a detention order.

Section 30. Powers of the Appellate Court

On appeal, the court may:
(a) confirm, modify, or revoke the detention order;
(b) impose, modify, or revoke conditions of release;
(c) order the release of the defendant;
(d) remand the matter for rehearing.

CHAPTER 7: MISCELLANEOUS PROVISIONS
================================================================================

Section 31. Compensation for Wrongful Detention

(a) A person who has been detained and subsequently acquitted, or whose
case has been dismissed, may apply to the court for compensation for the
period of detention.

(b) The court may award compensation taking into account:
    (1) the duration of the detention;
    (2) the circumstances of the arrest and detention;
    (3) the harm caused to the person;
    (4) any other relevant factor.

Section 32. Records and Documentation

(a) The police shall maintain records of all arrests, including the reason
for the arrest, the time of arrest, the time the arrested person was
brought before a judge, and the outcome of the hearing.

(b) The court shall maintain records of all detention hearings and orders.

Section 33. Regulations

The Minister of Justice may, with the approval of the Constitution, Law,
and Justice Committee of the Knesset, make regulations for the
implementation of this Law.

Section 34. Commencement

This Law shall come into force on the date determined by the Minister of
Justice, which shall be no later than six months from its publication.

SCHEDULE: OFFENSES CARRYING PRESUMPTION OF DANGEROUSNESS
(Referenced in Section 21(a)(3))
================================================================================

The following categories of offenses carry a presumption of dangerousness
for purposes of detention until the end of proceedings:

1. Offenses punishable by death or life imprisonment under the Penal Law,
   5737-1977, including:
   - Murder (Section 300)
   - Attempted murder (Section 305)
   - Manslaughter under aggravating circumstances

2. Security offenses under:
   - Prevention of Terrorism Ordinance, 5708-1948
   - Emergency Defense Regulations, 1945
   - Other security legislation as designated by the Minister of Justice

3. Drug trafficking offenses under the Dangerous Drugs Ordinance
   [New Version], 5733-1973:
   - Manufacturing, importing, exporting, or distributing dangerous drugs
   - Possession of dangerous drugs for purposes of distribution
   (Excluding: possession or use for personal consumption)

4. Offenses involving severe violence or cruelty:
   - Offenses committed with the use of a firearm or cold weapon
   - Offenses involving severe bodily harm
   - Offenses committed with particular cruelty

5. Domestic violence offenses:
   - Offenses of violence against a family member as defined in the
     Prevention of Violence in the Family Law, 5751-1991
   - In such cases, a presumption of dangerousness applies, which the
     defendant may rebut by demonstrating that release would not pose
     a danger

NOTES AND COMMENTARY
================================================================================

Legislative History:
- Original enactment: March 4, 1996 (Sefer HaChukkim 1558, p. 338)
- Major amendments include provisions on electronic monitoring (2014),
  expanded alternatives to detention, and enhanced protections for minors
  and vulnerable populations.

Constitutional Framework:
- This Law operates within the constitutional framework established by
  Basic Law: Human Dignity and Freedom (1992), which protects the right
  to personal liberty (Section 5) and requires that any limitation on
  rights satisfy the limitation clause (Section 8): enacted for a proper
  purpose, befitting the values of the State, and to an extent no greater
  than required (proportionality).

Key Supreme Court Interpretations:
- The Supreme Court has consistently held that detention is a severe
  infringement on the right to liberty and must be used only when
  strictly necessary.
- The duty to examine alternatives to detention (Section 21A) has been
  interpreted as a substantive obligation, not merely procedural.
- The presumption of dangerousness (Section 21(a)(3)) has been upheld
  but subject to proportionality review.
