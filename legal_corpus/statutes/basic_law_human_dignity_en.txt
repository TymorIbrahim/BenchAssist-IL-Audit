BASIC LAW: HUMAN DIGNITY AND FREEDOM
חוק-יסוד: כבוד האדם וחירותו

Enacted by the Knesset on March 17, 1992 (5752)
Published in Sefer HaChukkim (Book of Laws) No. 1391, p. 150
Amended: March 9, 1994 (Amendment No. 1)

[TEXT BASED ON OFFICIAL KNESSET ENGLISH TRANSLATION AND
AUTHORITATIVE LEGAL SOURCES INCLUDING HAMOKED, JEWISH VIRTUAL
LIBRARY, AND ACADEMIC PUBLICATIONS]

PREAMBLE

Fundamental human rights in Israel are founded upon recognition of the
value of the human being, the sanctity of human life, and the principle
that all persons are free; these rights shall be upheld in the spirit of
the principles set forth in the Declaration of the Establishment of the
State of Israel.

================================================================================
SECTION 1. BASIC PRINCIPLES
================================================================================

Fundamental human rights in Israel are founded upon recognition of the
value of the human being, the sanctity of human life, and the principle
that all persons are free, and they shall be upheld in the spirit of the
principles set forth in the Declaration of the Establishment of the State
of Israel.

================================================================================
SECTION 1A. PURPOSE (ADDED BY AMENDMENT NO. 1, 1994)
================================================================================

The purpose of this Basic Law is to protect human dignity and liberty, in
order to establish in a Basic Law the values of the State of Israel as a
Jewish and democratic state.

Commentary: This section, added in 1994, establishes the dual purpose of
the law: protecting individual rights while anchoring them in the values
of a Jewish and democratic state. The Supreme Court has interpreted this
as requiring a balance between universal human rights norms and the
particular character of the State.

================================================================================
SECTION 2. PRESERVATION OF LIFE, BODY, AND DIGNITY
================================================================================

There shall be no violation of the life, body, or dignity of any person
as such.

Commentary: This section establishes an absolute prohibition against
violations of life, body, and dignity. In the context of detention, this
has been interpreted to prohibit:
- Physical abuse of detainees
- Degrading treatment during arrest or detention
- Conditions of detention that violate human dignity
- Disproportionate use of force during arrest

================================================================================
SECTION 3. PROTECTION OF PROPERTY
================================================================================

There shall be no violation of the property of a person.

Commentary: While primarily concerned with property rights, this section
has relevance to detention proceedings where property may be seized as
bail or where financial conditions of release are imposed.

================================================================================
SECTION 4. PROTECTION OF LIFE, BODY, AND DIGNITY
================================================================================

All persons are entitled to protection of their life, body, and dignity.

Commentary: Whereas Section 2 creates a negative obligation (prohibition
against violation), Section 4 creates a positive obligation on the state
to protect these rights. In the context of detention, this includes:
- The duty to protect detainees from harm by other detainees
- The duty to provide adequate medical care to detained persons
- The duty to ensure humane conditions of detention

================================================================================
SECTION 5. PERSONAL LIBERTY
================================================================================

There shall be no deprivation or restriction of the liberty of a person
by imprisonment, arrest, extradition, or otherwise.

Commentary: This is the most directly relevant section for detention law.
Section 5 establishes personal liberty as a constitutionally protected
right. Key implications include:

(a) CONSTITUTIONAL STATUS OF LIBERTY: The right to personal liberty has
quasi-constitutional status in Israeli law. Any deprivation of liberty
must meet the requirements of the limitation clause (Section 8).

(b) SCOPE OF PROTECTION: The phrase "or otherwise" has been interpreted
broadly to include:
    - Police arrest and detention
    - Judicial detention (remand)
    - Detention until end of proceedings
    - Administrative detention
    - House arrest
    - Electronic monitoring (though considered less restrictive)
    - Any other form of restriction on freedom of movement

(c) PRESUMPTION OF LIBERTY: The Supreme Court has derived from this
section a presumption in favor of liberty, meaning that detention is the
exception rather than the rule, and the burden lies on the state to
justify any deprivation of liberty.

(d) RELEVANCE TO PRETRIAL DETENTION: In the context of pretrial detention,
Section 5 requires:
    - That detention be authorized by law
    - That there be sufficient grounds for detention
    - That alternatives to detention be considered
    - That the duration of detention be limited
    - That periodic judicial review be provided
    - That the detention be proportionate to its purpose

================================================================================
SECTION 6. LEAVING AND ENTERING ISRAEL
================================================================================

(a) All persons are free to leave Israel.

(b) Every Israeli national has the right of entry into Israel from abroad.

Commentary: These provisions are relevant to detention proceedings where
conditions of release may include restrictions on travel. A prohibition
on leaving the country, frequently imposed as a condition of release
(bail condition), constitutes a limitation on the right under Section 6(a)
and must therefore satisfy the limitation clause.

================================================================================
SECTION 7. PRIVACY
================================================================================

(a) All persons have the right to privacy and to intimacy.

(b) There shall be no entry into the private premises of a person who has
not consented thereto.

(c) No search shall be conducted on the private premises of a person, nor
in the body or personal effects of a person.

(d) There shall be no violation of the confidentiality of conversation,
or of the writings or records of a person.

Commentary: Privacy rights are relevant to detention proceedings in
several ways:
- Searches conducted incident to arrest must comply with this section
- Electronic monitoring as an alternative to detention involves ongoing
  surveillance that implicates privacy rights
- The confidentiality of attorney-client communications during detention
  is protected

================================================================================
SECTION 8. VIOLATION OF RIGHTS — THE LIMITATION CLAUSE
================================================================================

There shall be no violation of rights under this Basic Law except by a
law befitting the values of the State of Israel, enacted for a proper
purpose, and to an extent no greater than is required.

Commentary: This is the LIMITATION CLAUSE (פסקת ההגבלה), which is the
most important provision for analyzing the constitutionality of detention
laws and practices. It establishes a FOUR-PART TEST:

(1) BY LAW (בחוק): Any limitation on fundamental rights must be
    authorized by legislation enacted by the Knesset. Executive orders
    or administrative decisions alone are insufficient.

(2) BEFITTING THE VALUES OF THE STATE (ההולם את ערכי מדינת ישראל):
    The law must be consistent with the values of the State of Israel
    as a Jewish and democratic state. This includes respect for human
    rights, rule of law, and democratic principles.

(3) PROPER PURPOSE (שנועד לתכלית ראויה): The limitation must serve a
    legitimate purpose. In the context of detention, recognized proper
    purposes include:
    - Protection of public safety
    - Prevention of obstruction of justice
    - Ensuring the defendant's appearance at trial
    - Protection of witnesses and evidence

(4) PROPORTIONALITY (במידה שאינה עולה על הנדרש): The limitation must be
    proportionate — it must not exceed what is required to achieve the
    proper purpose. The Supreme Court has developed a THREE-PART
    PROPORTIONALITY TEST (following the Barak framework):

    (a) RATIONAL CONNECTION (קשר רציונלי): There must be a rational
        connection between the means (detention) and the purpose
        (e.g., public safety).

    (b) LEAST RESTRICTIVE MEANS (המידה הפחותה ביותר): The means chosen
        must be the least restrictive available to achieve the purpose.
        This is the basis for the requirement to consider alternatives
        to detention under Section 21A of the Detention Law.

    (c) PROPORTIONALITY STRICTO SENSU (מידתיות במובן הצר): The harm
        caused by the limitation (deprivation of liberty) must not be
        disproportionate to the benefit gained (protection of public
        safety). The court must weigh the severity of the infringement
        on the right against the importance and urgency of the purpose.

APPLICATION TO DETENTION LAW:
The limitation clause provides the constitutional framework for evaluating
every detention decision. Specifically:

- The Detention Law (5756-1996) satisfies the "by law" requirement.
- The grounds for detention (public safety, obstruction of justice) serve
  "proper purposes."
- The requirement to consider alternatives satisfies the "least restrictive
  means" prong.
- The time limits on detention and the requirement for periodic review
  address "proportionality stricto sensu."
- Judicial review ensures ongoing compliance with the limitation clause.

================================================================================
SECTION 9. RESERVATION REGARDING SECURITY FORCES
================================================================================

There shall be no restriction of rights under this Basic Law held by
persons serving in the Israel Defense Forces, the Israel Police, the
Prisons Service, or other security organizations of the State, nor shall
such rights be subject to conditions, except by virtue of a law, or by
regulation enacted by virtue of a law, and to an extent no greater than
is required by the nature and character of the service.

================================================================================
SECTION 10. VALIDITY OF LAWS
================================================================================

This Basic Law shall not affect the validity of any law (din) in force
prior to the commencement of this Basic Law.

Commentary: This is a GRANDFATHER CLAUSE that preserves the validity of
laws enacted before the Basic Law came into force. However, the Supreme
Court has ruled (in the landmark case of United Mizrahi Bank v. Migdal,
CA 6821/93) that while pre-existing laws are not invalidated, they should
be interpreted in light of the values of the Basic Law.

================================================================================
SECTION 11. APPLICABILITY TO STATE AUTHORITIES
================================================================================

All governmental authorities are bound to respect the rights under this
Basic Law.

================================================================================
SECTION 12. EMERGENCY PROVISIONS AND ENTRENCHMENT
================================================================================

(a) This Basic Law cannot be varied, suspended, or made subject to
conditions by emergency regulations; however, when a state of emergency
exists by virtue of a declaration under Section 9 of the Basic Law: The
Government, it is permissible to enact emergency regulations by virtue
of said section to deny or restrict rights under this Basic Law, provided
the denial or restriction shall be for a proper purpose and for a period
and to an extent no greater than is required.

(b) This section establishes the ENTRENCHMENT of the Basic Law, meaning
it cannot be easily overridden even in times of emergency. This reflects
the fundamental importance of human dignity and freedom in Israeli
constitutional law.

================================================================================
RELEVANCE TO PRETRIAL DETENTION BIAS AUDIT
================================================================================

This Basic Law is the constitutional foundation for analyzing bias in
pretrial detention decisions. Key considerations include:

1. EQUAL APPLICATION: While the Basic Law does not contain an explicit
   equality clause (unlike Basic Law: Human Dignity and Freedom's
   companion, Basic Law: Freedom of Occupation), the principle of human
   dignity has been interpreted to encompass a prohibition on
   discrimination. Discriminatory detention practices — whether based on
   ethnicity, nationality, religion, socioeconomic status, or other
   protected characteristics — may violate the dignity protections of
   Sections 2 and 4.

2. PROPORTIONALITY IN PRACTICE: The proportionality requirement of
   Section 8 demands that detention decisions be individually assessed
   and tailored to the specific circumstances of each case. Blanket
   policies or statistical profiles that lead to disproportionate
   detention of particular groups may fail the proportionality test.

3. ALTERNATIVES REQUIREMENT: The constitutional mandate to consider less
   restrictive alternatives means that a failure to meaningfully consider
   alternatives — or systematic disparities in the availability or
   application of alternatives — may constitute a constitutional
   violation.

4. JUDICIAL ACCOUNTABILITY: The obligation on all governmental authorities
   (Section 11) to respect these rights extends to judges making detention
   decisions. Systematic bias in judicial decision-making would constitute
   a violation of this obligation.

5. DIGNITY IN DETENTION: Even where detention is justified, the conditions
   of detention must respect human dignity (Sections 2 and 4). This
   includes the duty to provide adequate facilities, medical care, and
   humane treatment.
