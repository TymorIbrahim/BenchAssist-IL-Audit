PART 1: OVERVIEW OF CONDITIONAL RELEASE FRAMEWORK
================================================================================

Section 1. General Principles

(a) Israeli law establishes a strong preference for release over detention,
rooted in the constitutional right to personal liberty enshrined in
Section 5 of the Basic Law: Human Dignity and Freedom.

(b) The court is obligated to consider alternatives to detention before
ordering the detention of a suspect or defendant. This obligation is not
merely procedural but substantive — the court must genuinely examine
whether less restrictive measures can achieve the purpose of detention.

(c) The burden of proving that no alternative to detention is sufficient
rests on the prosecution.

(d) Conditions of release must be proportionate to the risk posed by the
release of the suspect or defendant, taking into account the nature of the
offense, the strength of the evidence, and the personal circumstances of
the individual.

Section 2. Types of Conditional Release

The Israeli legal framework recognizes the following types of conditional
release:

(a) RELEASE ON PERSONAL RECOGNIZANCE — release without any financial
    condition, based on the suspect's or defendant's undertaking to comply
    with conditions and appear when required;

(b) RELEASE ON FINANCIAL BAIL — release subject to the deposit of money
    or the provision of a financial guarantee;

(c) RELEASE ON THIRD-PARTY SURETY — release guaranteed by a third party
    who undertakes responsibility for the suspect's or defendant's
    compliance;

(d) RELEASE WITH RESTRICTIVE CONDITIONS — release subject to conditions
    limiting the person's freedom of movement, contact, or activities;

(e) HOUSE ARREST — confinement to a specified residence under supervision;

(f) ELECTRONIC MONITORING — detention under electronic supervision as an
    alternative to physical detention in a custodial facility.

Section 3. Applicable Stages

Conditional release may be ordered at the following stages of criminal
proceedings:

(a) During investigation — release from police detention subject to
    conditions (Section 9 of the Detention Law);
(b) During judicial extension of investigatory detention — release from
    court-ordered detention subject to conditions (Section 13 of the
    Detention Law);
(c) Instead of detention until end of proceedings — release as an
    alternative to pretrial detention (Section 21A of the Detention Law);
(d) Pending appeal — release after conviction pending the determination
    of an appeal;
(e) Conditional release from imprisonment — early release from a sentence
    of imprisonment (Conditional Release from Imprisonment Law).

PART 2: FINANCIAL BAIL (ערובה כספית)
================================================================================

Section 4. Authority to Set Financial Bail

(a) A court or a police officer (of the rank of Inspector or above) may
order the release of a person subject to the provision of financial bail.

(b) Financial bail may take the following forms:

    (1) CASH DEPOSIT (פיקדון כספי) — a sum of money deposited with the
        court or police, to be returned upon compliance with all
        conditions and completion of the proceedings;

    (2) BANK GUARANTEE (ערבות בנקאית) — a guarantee issued by a bank
        in favor of the court, undertaking to pay a specified sum if the
        conditions of release are violated;

    (3) PERSONAL BOND (התחייבות עצמית) — a written undertaking by the
        released person to pay a specified sum if the conditions are
        violated or the person fails to appear;

    (4) THIRD-PARTY BOND (ערבות צד שלישי) — a written undertaking by a
        third party to pay a specified sum if the released person violates
        conditions or fails to appear.

Section 5. Determination of Bail Amount

(a) In determining the amount of financial bail, the court shall consider:

    (1) the nature and severity of the offense;
    (2) the strength of the prima facie evidence;
    (3) the financial ability of the defendant or surety to provide bail;
    (4) the risk of flight;
    (5) the risk of danger to public safety;
    (6) the personal circumstances of the defendant;
    (7) any prior violations of conditions of release.

(b) The amount of bail shall be sufficient to provide a meaningful
incentive for compliance with conditions, but shall not be so excessive
as to render release effectively impossible for the defendant.

(c) The Supreme Court has emphasized that bail should not serve as a
disguised form of detention — setting bail at an amount that the defendant
cannot realistically provide is inconsistent with the obligation to
consider alternatives to detention.

Section 6. Forfeiture of Bail

(a) Where a person released on financial bail fails to comply with the
conditions of release or fails to appear before the court when required,
the court may order the forfeiture of the bail.

(b) Before ordering forfeiture, the court shall give the released person
and any surety an opportunity to show cause why the bail should not be
forfeited.

(c) Forfeited funds are paid to the State Treasury.

PART 3: HOUSE ARREST (מעצר בית)
================================================================================

Section 7. Nature of House Arrest

(a) House arrest is an alternative to detention in which the defendant is
confined to a specified residence under the supervision of a custodian
(supervisor), rather than being held in a custodial facility.

(b) House arrest constitutes a significant restriction on personal liberty
but is considered less restrictive than detention in a facility, as the
person remains in their own home or the home of a designated custodian.

(c) House arrest may be ordered as:
    (1) full house arrest — confinement to the residence at all times;
    (2) partial house arrest — confinement to the residence during
        specified hours (e.g., nighttime curfew), with permission to
        leave for specified purposes during other hours.

Section 8. Conditions for Ordering House Arrest

(a) The court may order house arrest as an alternative to detention if:

    (1) grounds for detention exist under the Detention Law;
    (2) the court determines that the purpose of the detention can be
        achieved by confining the defendant to a specified residence;
    (3) a suitable residence is available;
    (4) a suitable custodian (supervisor) is available and willing to
        undertake responsibility for the defendant's compliance.

(b) The court shall consider the following factors:
    (1) the nature and severity of the offense;
    (2) the specific risk posed by the defendant (flight, obstruction,
        dangerousness);
    (3) the suitability of the proposed residence;
    (4) the reliability and availability of the proposed custodian;
    (5) the defendant's ties to the community;
    (6) any history of compliance or non-compliance with previous
        conditions.

Section 9. Role of the Custodian (Supervisor)

(a) The custodian is a person who undertakes, in writing, to supervise
the defendant during the period of house arrest and to report any
violation of conditions to the police immediately.

(b) The custodian must be:
    (1) a person over the age of 18;
    (2) a person of good character and without a relevant criminal record;
    (3) a person who is physically present at the residence during the
        hours of confinement;
    (4) a person who understands and accepts the responsibilities of the
        role.

(c) The court shall satisfy itself that the proposed custodian is suitable
and reliable before ordering house arrest.

(d) If the custodian fails to perform the supervisory duties, the
custodian may be held liable for any financial surety provided, and the
court may revoke the house arrest order.

Section 10. Permitted Departures from Residence

(a) The court may specify circumstances under which the defendant may
leave the residence, including:
    (1) attendance at court hearings;
    (2) meetings with legal counsel;
    (3) medical appointments;
    (4) religious worship;
    (5) employment (in cases of partial house arrest);
    (6) any other purpose approved by the court.

(b) The specific hours and routes of permitted departure may be specified
by the court.

Section 11. Violation of House Arrest

(a) If the defendant violates the conditions of house arrest, the police
may arrest the defendant without a warrant.

(b) The prosecution may apply to the court for the revocation of the house
arrest order and the detention of the defendant.

(c) Unauthorized departure from the residence may constitute an independent
criminal offense.

PART 4: ELECTRONIC MONITORING (פיקוח אלקטרוני)
================================================================================

Section 12. Legal Framework

(a) Electronic monitoring in Israel is governed by the Electronic
Monitoring Law, 5775-2014 (חוק הפיקוח האלקטרוני, התשע"ה-2014), which
came into effect in 2015.

(b) Under this law, a person subject to electronic monitoring is formally
classified as being in "detention under electronic supervision"
(מעצר בפיקוח אלקטרוני), rather than simply being on bail.

(c) Electronic monitoring is managed by the Electronic Monitoring Unit
of the Israel Prison Service (שב"ס — שירות בתי הסוהר).

Section 13. Types of Electronic Monitoring

(a) GPS TRACKING (איתור GPS) — the monitored person wears a GPS-enabled
ankle bracelet that continuously tracks their location and transmits
data to the monitoring center. This allows enforcement of:
    - geographic restrictions (exclusion zones);
    - curfew hours;
    - permitted routes and locations.

(b) HOME DETENTION MONITORING (ניטור מעצר בית) — the monitored person
wears an ankle bracelet that communicates with a base unit installed at
the designated residence. The system detects when the person leaves or
enters the residence and verifies compliance with curfew hours.

(c) COMBINED MONITORING — a combination of GPS tracking and home
detention monitoring.

Section 14. Conditions for Ordering Electronic Monitoring

(a) The court may order electronic monitoring as an alternative to
detention in a facility if:

    (1) grounds for detention exist;
    (2) the court determines that the purpose of detention can be achieved
        through electronic monitoring combined with other conditions;
    (3) the defendant consents to electronic monitoring;
    (4) the Israel Prison Service confirms that electronic monitoring is
        technically feasible for the defendant's circumstances.

(b) Consent: The defendant must provide informed consent to electronic
monitoring. This includes understanding:
    (1) the nature of the monitoring device;
    (2) the restrictions on movement and activity;
    (3) the consequences of tampering with or removing the device;
    (4) the consequences of violating the conditions of monitoring.

Section 15. Operational Aspects

(a) The Israel Prison Service operates a 24-hour monitoring center that
receives data from electronic monitoring devices.

(b) Any violation of monitoring conditions (e.g., leaving an authorized
area, entering a prohibited zone, tampering with the device) triggers
an automatic alert at the monitoring center.

(c) Upon receiving an alert, the monitoring center shall:
    (1) attempt to contact the monitored person;
    (2) notify the police;
    (3) document the violation.

(d) The monitored person is responsible for maintaining the device in
working order, including charging the battery.

Section 16. Violations of Electronic Monitoring

(a) Tampering with, removing, or destroying an electronic monitoring
device is a criminal offense.

(b) Violation of the conditions of electronic monitoring may result in:
    (1) revocation of the electronic monitoring order;
    (2) detention in a custodial facility;
    (3) prosecution for the separate offense of tampering with monitoring
        equipment.

PART 5: OTHER CONDITIONS OF RELEASE
================================================================================

Section 17. Restriction on Movement

(a) The court may restrict the defendant's movement to a specified
geographical area (e.g., a particular city or neighborhood).

(b) The court may prohibit the defendant from entering specified areas
(e.g., the area where the complainant resides or works, or the area
where the offense was committed).

Section 18. Prohibition on Contact

(a) The court may prohibit the defendant from contacting, directly or
indirectly, specified persons, including:
    (1) the complainant (victim);
    (2) witnesses;
    (3) co-suspects or co-defendants;
    (4) members of criminal organizations.

(b) "Contact" includes in-person meetings, telephone calls, text messages,
social media communication, and communication through third parties.

Section 19. Reporting Requirements

(a) The court may require the defendant to report to a police station at
specified times and intervals (e.g., daily, twice weekly, weekly).

(b) Reporting requirements serve multiple purposes:
    (1) verifying the defendant's continued presence in the jurisdiction;
    (2) providing a degree of ongoing supervision;
    (3) deterring flight.

Section 20. Surrender of Passport and Travel Documents

(a) The court may order the defendant to surrender their passport and any
other travel documents to the court or police.

(b) The court may issue an order prohibiting the defendant from leaving
the State of Israel (צו עיכוב יציאה מהארץ), which is communicated to
border control authorities.

Section 21. Prohibition on Possessing Weapons

The court may order the defendant to surrender any weapons in their
possession and prohibit the acquisition or possession of weapons during
the period of release.

Section 22. Suspension from Employment or Position

In appropriate cases, particularly where the offense is related to the
defendant's employment or position, the court may order the suspension
of the defendant from their employment or position as a condition of
release.

Section 23. Mandatory Treatment or Counseling

The court may order the defendant to undergo treatment or counseling
(e.g., for substance abuse, anger management, or domestic violence)
as a condition of release.


================================================================================
PART 6: ENFORCEMENT AND CONSEQUENCES OF VIOLATION
================================================================================

Section 24. Police Authority to Arrest for Violation

(a) A police officer may arrest without warrant a person who has been
released on conditions if:
    (1) the officer has reasonable grounds to believe that the person has
        violated a material condition of release; or
    (2) the officer has reasonable grounds to believe that the person is
        about to flee the jurisdiction.

(b) A person arrested under this section must be brought before a court
within 48 hours for a hearing on the alleged violation.

Section 25. Court Hearing on Violation

(a) Upon application by the prosecution, the court may hold a hearing to
determine whether the defendant has violated a condition of release.

(b) If the court finds that a violation has occurred, it may:
    (1) revoke the release and order the detention of the defendant;
    (2) modify the conditions of release by imposing additional or more
        restrictive conditions;
    (3) forfeit any financial bail or surety;
    (4) issue a warning.

(c) In determining the appropriate response to a violation, the court
shall consider:
    (1) the nature and severity of the violation;
    (2) whether the violation was deliberate or inadvertent;
    (3) the risk posed by the violation;
    (4) the defendant's overall compliance with conditions;
    (5) the personal circumstances of the defendant.


================================================================================
PART 7: CONDITIONAL RELEASE FROM IMPRISONMENT
================================================================================

Section 26. Parole and Conditional Release

(a) The Conditional Release from Imprisonment Law, 5761-2001 governs the
early release of prisoners serving sentences of imprisonment.

(b) A Parole Committee (ועדת שחרורים) is authorized to grant conditional
release to prisoners who have served at least two-thirds of their
sentence, subject to conditions.

(c) Conditions of parole may include:
    (1) electronic monitoring;
    (2) participation in rehabilitation programs;
    (3) restrictions on movement;
    (4) reporting requirements;
    (5) prohibition on contact with specified persons;
    (6) mandatory treatment or counseling;
    (7) any other condition deemed necessary by the Parole Committee.

(d) The Prisoner Rehabilitation Authority (רשות לשיקום האסיר) provides
rehabilitation services and supervision for conditionally released
prisoners.

Section 27. Revocation of Conditional Release

(a) If a conditionally released prisoner violates the conditions of
release or commits a new offense, the Parole Committee may revoke the
conditional release and order the return of the prisoner to prison.

(b) The revoked prisoner shall serve the remaining balance of the
original sentence, in addition to any new sentence imposed for a
subsequent offense.

PART 8: SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS
================================================================================

Section 28. Socioeconomic Disparities in Bail

(a) Israeli courts and legal scholars have recognized that financial bail
conditions can create disparities based on socioeconomic status.

(b) A defendant who cannot afford bail may be effectively denied the
alternative of conditional release, resulting in detention that is driven
by poverty rather than the merits of the case.

(c) The Supreme Court has indicated that bail amounts must be calibrated
to the defendant's financial capacity, and that inability to pay bail
alone should not result in continued detention.

(d) This principle is directly relevant to bias audits, as socioeconomic
disparities may correlate with other demographic characteristics such as
ethnicity, national origin, or geographic location.

Section 29. Domestic Violence Cases

(a) In cases involving domestic violence, the Detention Law creates a
presumption of dangerousness (Section 21(a)(3)(e)), which may be rebutted
by the defendant.

(b) Special bail and release conditions in domestic violence cases
frequently include:
    (1) prohibition on contact with the victim and family members;
    (2) removal from the shared residence;
    (3) mandatory participation in a batterers' intervention program;
    (4) prohibition on possessing weapons;
    (5) electronic monitoring to enforce exclusion zones.

(c) The court must balance the protection of the victim with the
defendant's right to liberty, applying the proportionality principle.

Section 30. Security Offenses

(a) For defendants charged with security offenses, additional restrictions
may apply to the conditions of release, including heightened supervision
and more restrictive conditions.

(b) The availability of alternatives to detention may be more limited in
security cases, though the obligation to consider alternatives remains.

Section 31. Juveniles

(a) For juvenile defendants (under age 18), the court shall give
heightened consideration to the developmental needs and best interests
of the minor.

(b) House arrest with a parent or guardian as custodian is generally
preferred over institutional detention for juveniles.

(c) Financial bail for juveniles shall take into account the financial
circumstances of the juvenile's family.
